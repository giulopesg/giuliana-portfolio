01 — Origin

This started with
my own burnout.

In 2021, I hit burnout. I kept working — I couldn't stop — but something in my system had broken. I understood what needed to be done. I wanted to do it. And my body just wouldn't move.

I tried every productivity app I knew. Notion, Todoist, habit trackers. They made it worse. Every unchecked box felt like proof that I was failing. Every streak I broke felt like confirmation of a story I was already telling myself: that I couldn't sustain anything anymore.

Years later, I started using a Notion AI template to create small daily anchors for myself. It helped — but it was static, had no real intelligence, and couldn't adapt to how I was actually feeling. That gap became Neuroot.

"Uma âncora te impede de mover. Uma raiz te sustenta enquanto você cresce."

The philosophy behind the rebrand from Reanchor → Neuroot

The name evolved from Reanchor — a metaphor of passive stability — to Neuroot, a metaphor of active growth. Roots don't hold you still. They sustain you while you expand. That's the exact image of neuroplasticity: the nervous system rebuilding safe pathways through conscious repetition.

02 — The Problem

Productivity apps were designed
for people whose brains still work.

Burnout doesn't just cause tiredness. It causes neurological damage. Chronic stress elevates cortisol, which suppresses dopamine production in the mesolimbic system — the brain's reward circuit. The hippocampus loses the ability to distinguish real threats from false alarms. The amygdala becomes hyperactive.

The result: a person who knows exactly what they need to do, wants to do it, and whose nervous system treats every task as a survival threat.

What existing apps do What a recovering brain needs
Show backlogs of undone tasks One small action. No debt.
Punish broken streaks No punishment for absence
Measure volume, not safety Safety of effort, not output
Send pressure-based notifications Return to effort as a choice
03 — User Profiles

Three people.
One nervous system pattern.

⚡
The High-Performer in Transition

Laid off or burned out. Functionally paralyzed. Knows exactly what they should do. Their body refuses to start. Used to achieving — now afraid of effort itself.

🌫
The Hidden Functional

Still working, operating at 20% capacity. Looks fine from the outside. Inside, everything takes 10x the energy it should. Afraid to admit it.

🌱
The Post-Leave Returner

Medically dismissed, afraid of relapsing. Ready to try again — carefully. Needs a structure that won't punish them for going slowly.

04 — The Method

Behavioral Activation —
not productivity.

Neuroot is built on Behavioral Activation, a clinically validated approach from cognitive behavioral therapy. The core principle: repeated experiences of effort followed by reward — even small ones — rebuild the neural pathways that burnout damaged.

This is not gamification. It's not a habit tracker. It's a tool that teaches the nervous system that acting won't destroy you again.

The dual philosophical base: neuroplasticity (the brain rebuilds safe pathways through repetition) meets Kung Fu philosophy (the middle path — neither excess nor deprivation). Both systems arrived at the same truth: conscious repetition transforms who you are. This philosophy is implicit in the product — users feel it without reading it.

"Teach the nervous system that effort is safe. One practice. One day. Stop before you're tired. That's the whole method."

The single goal of Neuroot
What the app deliberately doesn't do
✕ No streaks or consecutive-day counting
✕ No notifications that mention absence
✕ No visible backlog of unfinished days
✕ No aggressive colors (red/orange removed)
✕ No gamification of any kind

AI Executes

Discovery & PRD — I started with the Notion template I was already using for myself. That template became the first prototype — its limitations became the brief. I documented the problem, user profiles, scientific foundation, no-gos, rabbit holes, and success indicators using the Shape Up methodology.

Design with Figma Make — I used Figma Make to prototype all screens at high fidelity. The visual language emerged from the methodology: no aggressive colors, no red alerts, no urgency. A watercolor tree as the core illustration — rooted, alive, growing slowly.

Development with Claude Code — I built the full app using Claude Code — writing prompts, reviewing code, iterating on behavior — without a technical co-founder. Stack: React + TypeScript, Supabase for auth and database, GitHub for version control, Vercel for deployment.

AI in the onboarding — The Claude API powers a conversational onboarding. Instead of a form, the app opens with a question: "What did you used to do with ease, but now your body freezes just thinking about?" The AI generates personalized practices from the user's answers — already named, timed, and written in the product's voice.

06 — Key Screens & Decisions

Every word is a
product decision.

Microcopy is product strategy here. The language is the therapy.

01
Home — Empty State

"There's no practice defined for today." — Not "You haven't done anything yet." The language removes debt. One CTA: "Choose today's practice" — an invitation, never a task.

Copy decision: the app cannot know you were absent. It only knows you came back.
02
Execution — Timer

Full-screen timer. Limit visible. At the bottom: "Stop when the time ends — even if you haven't finished. Stopping before exhaustion is part of the method."

Button label: "End at the right point." — Not "Done." This is the method in a button label.
03
Post-Practice Registration

Three axes: Body (Light / Neutral / Heavy), Mind (Clear / Neutral / Tired), Resistance (Less / Same / More). No scores. No percentages. Just observation.

Design decision: data tracks nervous system recalibration over time without turning it into performance metrics.
04
Practice Completed

"Você fez sua prática hoje." Then: "Executing beyond the plan doesn't accelerate — it only teaches the body that limits don't exist."

The screen actively discourages doing more. That's the product. No buttons. Just presence.
05
Week View

Shows what happened, never what should have happened. The single weekly question: "Did your body feel less resistant to effort this week?"

Design decision: that's the only metric that matters. Nervous system recalibration, not streak count.
06
AI Onboarding

No forms. No signup before understanding what the product does. The AI asks one question at a time, responds with brief empathetic context, and generates 3 personalized practices.

The user sees their practices before they ever see the home screen.
07 — Learnings

What building this
taught me.

1
The hardest part wasn't design or code — it was the PRD.
Defining what the product truly was — and wasn't — took longer than anything else. Every feature idea had to be tested against the core question: does this teach the nervous system that effort is safe, or does it do the opposite?
2
Microcopy is product strategy.
"End at the right point" instead of "Done." "You've done what you promised" instead of "Congratulations." Every word in this app is a decision about how the brain will receive it. Language is the therapy.
3
Building alone with AI is a new design skill.
I wrote the prompts, reviewed the output, directed the iterations, and caught the errors. The ability to think in product terms and translate that into precise instructions for AI — that's a skill that didn't exist before this project became possible.
4
Personal experience is a valid research source.
I was my own first user. That's not a limitation — it's a foundation. The product has depth because the problem has depth. The PRD was grounded in real behavioral evidence, not assumed pain points.
